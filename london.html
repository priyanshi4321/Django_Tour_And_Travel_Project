Must See Places in London

British Museum

Founded in 1753, it is the world's first national museum with 6 million visitors a year. You can see ancient artefacts from all over the world here. The entire interior is open for free, but if you listen to it with the guide, you can enjoy it in more detail and easily and in detail.

London Eye

London Eye is the largest Ferris wheel in Europe at 135 meters. The Thames River in London, overlooking the London Eye Ferris wheel, is really beautiful. It is one of London's landmarks with 3.5 million visitors a year.

Madame Tussauds

The closest way to see the royal family is to visit the Madame Tussauds Museum. Here you can see the Queen's wax figure. Also, you can see a wax sculpture that realistically represents British celebrities in the museum. It is also fun to test how many soccer players and movie stars your friends know in this educational and enjoyable place.

Big Ben

It's a giant landmark that symbolizes London. Definitely it is One of top 10 places must see in London. It is the grandest building with the largest clock in the world. This is called the Big Ben, but the official name of Big Ben in London is the Elizabeth Tower, which was named in commemoration of the 60th anniversary of Elizabeth II's accession in 2010.

Buckingham Palace

This is the Royal Palace of England where Queen Elizabeth II lives in England. Buckingham Palace is a palace representing the British royal family, and the interior is decorated with beautiful ornaments. Usually, Queen Elizabeth II performs state affairs. Also don't miss the outside guard rotation!

Westminster Abbey

You have to pay an entrance fee to enter, but the atmosphere inside is worth it. Right here in Westminster, there is a royal wedding, including Prince William, the Duke of Cambridge. Go on a pilgrimage to the poet's corner where Tennyson, Kipling, Hardy, and Handel are asleep. And don't forget to look for ten modern martyrs decorating the upper wall of the Great West Door.

Famous Cuisine in London

Bangers and Mash

First of all,bangers are sausages,just so we’re all on the same page.They’re called that due to the way they used to explode out of their skins while being fried.The British have loved pork products for ages—ever since the days when we used to gauge the size of a forest by how many swine we could fit in it.Despite recent changes in eating habits,this is still one of the most popular traditional foods in London.It’s best when paired with a pile of buttery mash and an unctuous onion gravy.

Scotch Egg

This is a staple of English snacks: a boiled egg, surrounded by pork meat, coated in breadcrumbs and fried. It’s found everywhere from street food stalls to gas stations. Legend has it that the Scotch egg was invented by the high-end grocery store Fortnum & Mason to be a portable snack for travelers. Whatever its origins, this spherical beauty is a true taste of England, and certainly one of our favorite traditional foods in London.

Fish and Chips

The history of fish and chips reveals a surprising origin story. Fried fish came from the Jews exiled from the Iberian Peninsula during the 1400s, and chips from French-speaking Belgians. The only thing we did was pair them together for the first time. But they became such an important part of the national psyche that they were one of the few things not rationed during World War II. The best place for this dish? An actual fish and chip shop—not a pub!

Chicken Tikka Masala

With a huge South Asian population, London is one of the best places in the world for Indian and Pakistani foods. Chicken tikka masala is the poster child for the Anglo-Indian cuisine that took the country by storm after the days of the British Empire.The allure of Chicken Tikka Masala lies in its harmonious blend of flavors and textures. Tender pieces of marinated chicken are first grilled to perfection, imparting a smoky char that adds depth to the dish.

Roast Dinner

You would be hard pressed to find a Brit who doesn’t feel a nostalgic pull toward our Sunday tradition: a full roast dinner with all the trimmings, surrounded by friends and family. Choose your meat and prepare to be comforted by roast potatoes, veggies, Yorkshire pudding and cauliflower cheese with lashings of gravy. In other words, several of the best traditional foods in London piled on one plate.

Sticky Toffee Pudding

Considering that the English are known as the “pudding-eaters,” it’s ironic that one of our most famous puddings is probably not from England at all. In fact, it’s not even a pudding in the classic sense; its preparation is closer to that of a muffin. Sticky toffee pudding was first brought to England by Canadian pilots in World War II. Not that we mind—when a dessert is this good, we’re more than happy to claim it as our own.

Spaghetti Bolognese

What’s an Italian dish doing on this list, you ask? We hear you…but this is no more authentically Italian than Alfredo. Admittedly, the original Bolognese is a ragu from Bologna. But it tastes nothing like the sauce we heap onto our spaghetti (which is absolutely not the pasta shape it should be paired with). Having left plenty of Italian chefs and tourists wringing their hands in dismay, “spag bol” remains the epitome of English comfort food.

Full English Breakfast

A culinary relic of our Anglo-Saxon roots, this dish became popular among all socioeconomic classes during the Industrial Revolution. It’s a cuddle on a plate, perfect for the morning after a big night out or to set you up for a long day of work. Head to a classic “caff” and go for the full works: sausage, bacon, baked beans, tomato, fried egg, fried slice and of course, a hefty slice of black pudding.
